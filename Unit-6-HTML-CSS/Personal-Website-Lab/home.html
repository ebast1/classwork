Blogging

Start with Curiosity

Every project begins with a question. What problem am I really trying to solve? Who is this for? Why does it matter? Curiosity fuels everything I do. I don't just want to know what a client or user needs—I want to understand it well enough to build something that anticipates what they didn’t know they needed.

Process Over Perfection

I've learned that iteration beats hesitation. Early in my journey, I used to overthink every step before committing to a direction. Now, I believe in putting something into the world—even if it's rough—and shaping it through feedback and reflection. Progress is built in layers, and I embrace every version along the way.

Creating with Care

Whether it's a visual detail or a small interaction, the little things matter. I try to build with care, because thoughtful design (and development) often reveals itself in the moments that are easy to overlook. At the end of the day, if something I make makes someone pause—even briefly—to appreciate the experience, I consider that a win.

Developing

Currently Developing

Lately, I've been focused on sharpening my skills in HTML, CSS, C#, and SwiftUI. I'm especially interested in how more streamlined UX can lead to more inclusive, efficient, or meaningful experiences.

Exploration

Outside of structured work, I've been researching Design principles and how they connect to the work I already do and where it might take me next. I don't believe learning ever really stops, and this space is where I let curiosity lead.

Hometown

Where's my Hometown?

I grew up between Dearborn and Grosse Ile, which are two totally different vibes, but both feel like home in their own way. Dearborn is where I got a sense of how much is always going on—different people, ideas, cultures, food spots, history, all packed into one place. It's busy, grounded, and always kind of pushing forward. I think being around that made me curious early on. It's probably where I picked up my love for figuring things out and making stuff that actually matters to people. Grosse Ile is slower, quieter—it's literally an island. That's where I spent time just wandering around, playing games, sketching ideas, or doing nothing and somehow still learning from it. It's where I learned to enjoy the process and not just chase the finish line.
